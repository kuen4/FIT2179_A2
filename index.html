Are Australians Getting What They Pay For?

An investigation into broadband speeds, promises, and the reality of internet performance across Australia, using independent test data from 1,148 households.

Data: ACCC Measuring Broadband Australia Report 32 (Dec 2025) · NBN Co via data.gov.au

Setting the scene

Australia's National Broadband Network connects over 8 million premises across every state and territory. The rollout, which began in around 2016 took years and utilised a mixed technology model, deploying fibre, wireless, and satellite connections depending on the location. Are Providers Delivering?

Retail Service Providers (RSPs) like Telstra, Optus, and Aussie Broadband advertise maximum theoretical speeds, but real world network congestion means performance drops, especially during the 7–11 PM busy hours when most households are streaming, gaming, and video calling. The question is: which providers deliver closest to what they promise, and which fall short?

The technology lottery

Your broadband experience depends heavily on the type of NBN connection at your address which is something most consumers have no control over. Fibre to the Premises (FTTP) delivers the best results because the fibre optic cable runs directly to your home. Fibre to the Node (FTTN), by contrast, relies on old copper telephone wiring for the final stretch, and consistently underperforms. Where you live matters too, as rural and remote areas face a significant speed gap compared to urban centres.

The bigger picture

Beyond download speeds, upload performance and latency paint a fuller picture of broadband quality. Uploads matter for video calls, cloud backups, and working from home and unlike downloads, NBN Co does not overprovision them. Latency affects everything from online gaming to video conferencing. The map below combines speed, latency, and sample size into one view, while the radar chart scores each provider across four key dimensions to reveal who truly performs best overall.

Provider scorecard: who performs best overall?

Each provider scored across four dimensions. Values are normalised (0-100) so axes are comparable. Use the dropdown to compare providers.

How to read this: Each axis represents a key performance metric. A larger, more symmetrical "diamond" shape indicates a provider that delivers a high-quality experience across all categories, rather than just raw speed.

The Findings: While providers like Optus and TPG lead in raw download speeds, they often show lower scores in stability or upload performance.

Aussie Broadband and Exetel emerge as the most balanced performers. Their diamonds are the most symmetric, indicating they provide high reliability and stability without sacrificing the speed required for modern household needs.
